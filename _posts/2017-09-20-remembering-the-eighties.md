# Remembering the eighties
{% include post_header.html %}
{% include defs.md %}
As one of the most intense events, I still remember the weekend of August, 27th and the following 
day, the 28th.

On August, 27th, very early in the morning, we (my father, my elder brother and myself) hopped into 
the car for a journey, I'll never forget. Our first target was the city of [Schweinfurt in 
Bavaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schweinfurt), Germany, where the 1988 Monsters of Rock 
festival was going to take place. I do not remember much of the drive, because I slept most of the 
time, but I know, the 400 mile drive took us around 7 hours, and we arrived in Schweinfurt around 
noon. The situation was "chaotic" and that's some understatement. Close to 50.000 metal fans do have 
some impact, even on a medium sized city and we heard, some savage idiots caused chaos and mayhem 
the evening before, making it into the headlines on all German news networks. 

All in all, it appeared peaceful, but a lot of people were already totally drunk and/or stoned at 
noon, long before the first band was scheduled to play. I was totally excited, because I was going 
to see one of my favorite bands, **Iron Maiden**, for the first time ever in a live gig, but because 
of my age, alcohol was a no-go. My dad was usually a tolerant person, but this was something he 
didn't want to see and he told my older brother to "*watch it*".

My dad didn't want to attend the gig, but had planned to find a bed and some food at a small hotel 
in a nearby village, owned by an old friend of him and we agreed on a meeting point on a large 
parking space near the festival grounds later in the evening.

When Iron Maiden finally went off stage, it was already dark, but I don't remember the exact time. 
It took us at least one hour to reach the meeting point where our father was already waiting for us, 
even though it wasn't a long walk, but there were so many people, many of them totally drunk and it 
was really a huge mess. My brother, tall and fairly well in shape, looked after me, so it wasn't a 
problem.

When we finally reached the agreed meeting point on the parking space, my dad was there, waiting. He 
looked fresh and told us he had slept well and agreed with his friend at the hotel to get us all 
some special service in form of late night food. I remember, I realized I had not eaten anything 
since the breakfast at home, some 18 hours or so earlier. I had been way too excited to be hungry.

[...]

We arrived at our father's friend's hotel around midnight (I guess, could have been later) after a 
relatively short drive of which I do not remember much. The hotel, I remember quite clearly though; 
It was a small hotel (often called a pension in Germany) with only a couple of rooms, ran by my 
father's friend and his wife (both of them, I had never met before). They were friendly and humorous 
folk and the food was excellent, but strangely, the thing I remember most is, how extremely tired I 
was. I had not been up for an extended period of time, maybe just 14 hours, and I had slept enough 
before, but I was quite exhausted from the gig.

Soon after we finished this midnight diner and took a shower at around 2am, my father told us we had 
about 2 hours left, until we had to get ready for the next stage of our journey, a 200 mile travel 
to the city of Ramstein in the Rhineland area, where the Flugtag 88 was going to to take place on 
the US Air Force base. Enough time for a short nap, which we both thankfully took even though I 
knew, I would be sleeping in the car anyway for most of the upcoming drive.

This drive, which would normally take approximately 3 hours, took at least 5, possibly more, because 
of major traffic jams around the air force base, but like on the day before, I had slept most of the 
time during the drive. My brother, who later told me, he didn't sleep so well, occupied the front 
seat next to my dad, so I had all the backseats of our comfortable series 5 BMW for myself - nice 
and spacey enough for a good sleep.

The Flugtag was a huge event, with more than 150.000 spectators expected and probably close to 
300.000 attending. We finally arrived on the location around 11am, quite late and the show had 
already opened its doors, but the most interesting part, the flying air show displays, was about to 
start in the afternoon, so there was still enough time left to walk around, get some burgers, see 
all the cool aircraft on ground display and enjoy a beautiful, hot, late summer day.

At around 3:30pm, we decided to head towards the exit, because our plan was to reach our car early 
in order to avoid the massive traffic jam that we expected. Minutes later, the Italian display team 
Frecche Tricolori was announced as one of the highlights and I think, they were the last flying 
display on schedule. We stopped to watch them, because my dad told us they were awesome and the only 
team flying with 10 aircraft. The show was indeed spectacular as promised and we watched for 
minutes, standing in a position at least 150 yards away from the place, that was soon going to turn 
into hell on earth. I later realized that it was **pure luck** we didn't get affected by the 
dreadful accident. We *had* been near the ice cream truck that got fully hit by the cartwheeling 
aircraft maybe an hour before, and we *had* been close to the place where the white station wagon 
was parked near the perimeter road. That place took a direct hit and people standing there had 
little chance to survive.

So we watched that big heart painted into the steel blue sky, the maneuver that was meant to show 
love and was announced as the closing event.

What then happened, I do not fully remember. I do remember a strange, metallic sound, followed by a 
loud "thump". I *do* remember there was a lot of fire and smoke and I *believe*, I was able to feel 
heated air and some kind of pressure wave that hurt my ears. I also *believe*, my father let loose a 
loud vulgar curse before everything started to turn into a nightmare, but he said later, he wasn't 
able to remember. He, being a pilot (although not a military one), might have realized that they 
were going to crash mid-air. Or maybe, it wasn't real and I fantasized. I dunno.

And I remember lots of people, including ourselves, running, screaming names, but my dad quickly 
realized, there was no imminent danger for us and he yelled us to stop and stick together.

## Looking back

When I look back, I'm not even sure if everything was real. If there weren't for the many videos, 
pictures, reports from other people, which - as a rational thinking being - I accept as being real, 
I would have doubts. Sometimes, it feels like a bad dream, or a Hollywood movie. The images of the 
large fire pretty much looked like from an action movie and some of these pictures are still 
surprisingly clear even though everything happened 30 years ago.

I also remember very little from what happened after the event. At some point, we reached our car 
and there was a massive traffic chaos, but I cannot remember details from our drive home which took 
us many hours, probably 10 or more. Later I was told it could have been some kind of mild shock. 
Even I was only fourteen years old, I very likely realized that a lot of people got hurt without 
seeing reports on TV or in newspapers. The chaos, the screams, the smoke clouds, the smell (burned 
stuff), the lots of military guys, some armed, the ambulances, the overall noise level, it all might 
have contributed.

What I *clearly* remember is my mom when we finally arrived home - she was in tears and couldn't 
stop crying for hours. Even though my dad called her on phone as soon as we found a working phone 
box (there were no mobile phones back then) and told her we were all fine and heading home, she 
probably didn't realize that we didn't get hurt until we finally arrived. She *had* seen the reports 
on TV *after* my dad phoned home, but that didn't help much.
